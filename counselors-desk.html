"New Empathy" in Middle Schoolers

Credits: Jessica Luna  ·  IB MYP Educators' Resource

Middle school is one of the most emotionally turbulent phases of a young person's life. As IB MYP educators, we are uniquely positioned not just to teach, but to model and nurture the kind of empathy our students will carry into the world.

The graphic below draws a powerful distinction. The old empathy was about absorbing others' emotions — losing yourself in them, trying to feel exactly what someone else feels. The new empathy is more grounded and more sustainable. It is about presence, validation, and intentional support.

The Distinction
✦ The New Empathy
  • Being present enough to feel the energy of the room
  • Recognising that emotions are temporary & allowing processing space/time
  • Accepting your power & responsibility to simply listen
  • Respecting others' experience (even if you don't understand first-hand)
  • Validating others' emotions PLUS thoughts, feelings, perceptions
  • Offering platinum support — asking what support they need
✦ The Old Empathy
  • Losing your self-control in others' emotions
  • Focusing only on others' emotions
  • Trying to feel others' emotions yourself
  • Ignoring established boundaries
  • Thinking you're not empathetic because you've had different experiences
Here is how we can bring this into our classrooms & pastoral practice
In the Classroom
01

Be Present

Before diving into a lesson, take thirty seconds to genuinely notice how your students are showing up that day. A simple "How is everyone doing today?" signals that you see them as whole people, not just learners.

Validate emotions without fixing them. When a student is upset or overwhelmed, resist the instinct to immediately solve the problem. Acknowledge first — "That sounds really hard" — before moving into solutions. This directly mirrors the "new empathy" principle of validating thoughts, feelings, and perceptions together.

Recognise that emotions are temporary. In MYP, students face real academic pressure. Normalise the idea that stress, frustration, and anxiety are passing states, not permanent identities. Build brief emotional check-ins or reflection moments into your weekly rhythm.

In Pastoral Support
02

Ask, Don't Assume

The concept of platinum support — asking what kind of support someone needs rather than deciding for them — is transformative in a pastoral setting. Train yourself and your students to ask:

"Would you like a suggestion, or do you just need me to listen right now?"

Create psychological safety in your tutor group. Students need to know that their boundaries will be respected and their experiences won't be dismissed just because others haven't shared them. Regularly affirm that different backgrounds and different feelings are all equally valid.

Developing Empathy as a Skill

IB Learner Profile Connection

Empathy in the IB framework connects directly to the Learner Profile attribute of being caring, but it also underpins open-mindedness and balance. Consider weaving these conversations into:

  • Tutor time: short weekly discussions on real social scenarios
  • ATL skill-building: social and communication skills explicitly taught, not assumed
